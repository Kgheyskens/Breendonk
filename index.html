The History of Breendonk

Our field trip to Fort Breendonk was not just a visit to a museum, it was a confrontation with the reality of the Nazi occupation in Belgium. Located near Antwerp, this fortification became a symbol of terror during World War II. Below is the lifeline of the fort, tracing its transformation from a defensive structure to a camp of horror.

1906-1914

The Defense

Fort Breendonk was constructed as a key component of the "National Redoubt," a defensive belt of fortresses designed to protect the vital port of Antwerp and provide a fallback position for the Belgian Army. Its thick concrete walls and moat, state-of-the-art for their time, were intended to withstand heavy artillery. The fort represented a symbol of national security, engineered to safeguard Belgium's sovereignty against foreign invasion.

September 1940

The Occupation

After the German invasion in May 1940, the fort was quickly repurposed by the Nazi regime. The Sicherheitspolizei-Sicherheitsdienst (SIPO-SD), the Nazi security police, established it as an "Auffanglager" or reception camp. Its primary function was to imprison political dissidents, resistance fighters, Jews, and other individuals deemed "enemies of the Reich." The very structure built to protect Belgians was turned into an instrument of their oppression.

1940 – 1944

The Prisoners

Over its four years of operation, approximately 3,600 prisoners passed through Breendonk's gates. They were a diverse group: left-wing political opponents, captured members of the armed resistance, Allied airmen, and a significant number of Jews who were held here before being deported to Auschwitz. Prisoners were stripped of their names and given numbers, a systematic process of dehumanization. They were forced to wear worn-out Belgian army uniforms, a cruel irony given the fort's original purpose.

Daily Life, 1940 – 1944

Terror & Torture

Daily existence in Breendonk was a calculated regime of starvation, forced labor, and psychological terror. Prisoners were forced to perform grueling, pointless tasks, such as moving earth by hand. Malnutrition was rampant, with daily rations consisting of little more than watery soup and a small piece of bread. The camp was infamous for its torture chamber, where the SS and Flemish collaborators used brutal methods to extract information. Executions by hanging or firing squad were common, serving as a constant warning to the other inmates.

1942 – 1944

The Ultimate Sacrifice

The execution grounds remain one of the most chilling parts of the fort. Here, 164 prisoners were executed by firing squad, and 21 were hanged. The gallows and the execution posts stand as silent witnesses to the ultimate price paid by those who dared to resist. These executions were often public within the camp, designed to break the spirit of the remaining prisoners.

September 1944

Liberation

As Allied forces advanced through Belgium, the Nazis hastily abandoned Fort Breendonk, taking the remaining prisoners with them on a "death march" to other camps. The fort was officially liberated on September 4, 1944. What the liberators found was a chilling, almost perfectly preserved site of torture and death. In 1947, it was declared a national memorial, ensuring that the suffering and resilience of its victims would never be forgotten and that the fort would stand as a permanent lesson for future generations.

1944 – 1947

Breendonk II: The Repression

History is rarely black and white. After the liberation, the fort was used by the Belgian state to imprison collaborators, "incivieken," and Germans. This period, known as "Breendonk II," saw the roles reversed. While intended as justice, the conditions were initially harsh, driven by the intense emotions and desire for revenge that followed the occupation. It serves as a reminder of the complex and often painful aftermath of war.

How it was

Walking through the cold corridors of Breendonk leaves a mark on you. It forces you to ask difficult questions about humanity, cruelty, and your own place in history.

What Touched Me

The most memorable part was the sheer coldness of the stone and the silence. Standing in the dormitories, imagining hundreds of men cramped together, starving and terrified, made me feel a heavy sadness. It wasn't just history anymore; it felt real. The visit made me feel small but also grateful for the freedom I often take for granted.

Lessons Learned

I learned that evil isn't always a monster, sometimes it's a system. The way the camp was organized, the bureaucracy of death, was unexpected. I would have liked to learn more about the individual stories of resistance in school, because seeing it in person connects you emotionally in a way a textbook never could. Although I've visited a lot of monuments and memorials from the WOII times I didn't really expect what I've seen here.

Personal Connection

This experience connects to my personal growth by showing me the importance of resilience. It changes the way I look at the world and I realize that peace is fragile, and it demands constant vigilance from each of us. It also shows me that the freedom we have now and the luxuery of these times makes us slowly forget what people, back in the days, had to go through.

Did We Improve?

Have we learned from history? In some ways, yes... we have international laws and human rights now. But we still see hatred and war in the world. We could improve by being more vigilant and speaking up against injustice early, before it turns into something like Breendonk or even just WOII. Remembrance is the only vaccine we have against repeating the past and remembering the ones we've lost.

A personal touch

I also feel a strange connection with this. My great-grandfather served for the Germans during the war as a morter. Knowing that my own family history is intertwined with this conflict makes the experience even more complex and profound for me, for me it's more a reminder that history is never purely distant, but it lives on in the people we come from.

A view in the Future

"Those who cannot remember the past are condemned to repeat it."

As we leave the gates of Fort Breendonk behind, we must not leave the lessons there. The story of Breendonk is a warning, but it is also a call to action. It teaches us that we must actively protect our values of democracy and tolerance.

The future is not written yet. It is up to us to ensure that it is a future of peace, understanding, and respect for every human life. By remembering the victims of Breendonk, we honor them not just with sorrow, but with a promise: Never Again.
